OPTIONS 101 · EDUCATION ONLY

Direction is only
the first decision.

An option is a time-limited contract. You are trading direction, magnitude, volatility, and time—all at once. Start with defined-risk purchases before studying option selling.

BUY A CALLBULLISH ↗

The right to buy shares at the strike.

You want the stock above the strike plus the premium by expiration. If it does not get there, the contract can expire worthless.

Maximum loss
Premium paid + fees
Expiration breakeven
Strike + premium
Upside
Theoretically unlimited
BUY A PUTBEARISH / HEDGE ↘

The right to sell shares at the strike.

You want the stock below the strike minus the premium by expiration. A put can also hedge shares you already own.

Maximum loss
Premium paid + fees
Expiration breakeven
Strike − premium
Maximum gain
If stock falls to $0
Contract anatomyWhat every quote means
01
Contract multiplier

One standard equity option usually controls 100 shares. A $2.50 quoted premium generally costs $250 per contract.

02
Strike price

The fixed price at which the underlying shares may be bought with a call or sold with a put.

03
Expiration

The contract’s deadline. Time value generally erodes as expiration approaches; a correct direction can still lose money if the move is too small or too late.

04
Exercise & assignment

The buyer has a right. The seller accepts an obligation. Assigned calls can require selling shares; assigned puts can require buying shares.

05
IV and theta

Implied volatility affects what you pay. Theta describes time decay. Both can move an option even when the stock barely moves.
